## 0. STOP — DO WE EVEN HAVE BED BUGS?

Before spending money on exterminators, encasements, or any of the rest: **confirm the diagnosis first.** Ben already suspects she may not actually have them, and that suspicion deserves a serious 24-hour investigation before treating this as a bed bug emergency.

### What "actually having bed bugs" looks like (per UMN Extension, EPA, Cornell IPM)

You need at least ONE of these — bites alone are not enough:

1. **Live bugs** — apple-seed sized, flat, reddish-brown adults; or smaller translucent nymphs.
2. **Dark fecal spots on sheets, mattress seams, or walls** — like ballpoint-pen ink dots; smear when wiped with a wet paper towel.
3. **Pale shed exoskeletons** — look like tiny empty bug shells in mattress seams or bedframe joints.
4. **Pearl-white eggs** — ~1 mm, stuck in cracks (rare to see).
5. **Sweet musty odor** — only in heavy infestations.

### What gets misdiagnosed as bed bugs (especially in seniors)

This is critical. Studies and dermatology literature consistently show that a high percentage of self-diagnosed "bed bug" cases are actually:

- **Dry skin / xerosis** — extremely common in seniors, causes itchy red patches that look like bites.
- **Scabies** — mites that burrow under skin; itching especially at night; needs prescription treatment, not pest control.
- **Allergic reactions** — to detergent, fabric softener, a new lotion, or medication.
- **Folliculitis or contact dermatitis** — from skin friction, fabric, or detergent.
- **Flea bites** — usually around ankles/lower legs; possible if she's had recent contact with any animal.
- **Mosquito or other insect bites** — Florida and other warm states.
- **Delusional parasitosis** — a real, treatable medical condition more common in seniors with anxiety or cognitive changes; the person genuinely *feels* bugs that aren't there. Not "making it up" — a true neurological symptom.

### The 24-hour confirmation protocol (do this BEFORE Thursday)

**MOM-FACING:**

> **Step 0-M-1.** Take your phone. Pull all the sheets and pillowcases off the current bed. Take a close-up photo of: the mattress seams (the piped edges), the corners of the box spring, and inside the bed frame joints. Send to Ben.
>
> **Step 0-M-2.** Take photos of any bites you have — close up, with good light, ideally near a coin or finger for size scale. Send to Ben.
>
> **Step 0-M-3.** When you wake up tomorrow, before getting up, look at the sheets — any small dark dots? Any tiny bug? Take pictures. Send to Ben.

**BEN'S 24-HOUR CHECKLIST:**

1. Review the photos. Are there fecal spots? Cast skins? Live bugs? If yes → proceed with the plan below.
2. Are bites in the classic "breakfast/lunch/dinner" linear pattern of 3 in a row, on exposed skin (arms, legs, neck)? Vs. random distribution / concentrated on lower legs (fleas) / between fingers and on wrists (scabies).
3. **Cheap option:** Pay for a single professional **inspection** (not treatment) — usually $75–$200. Some companies bring a trained dog. This is the rational thing to spend money on if uncertain. NOT a $1,000 treatment.
4. **Free option:** Many county extension offices (USDA Cooperative Extension Service) will identify a sample bug for free. Mom tapes one to a piece of paper, mails it in, gets a written ID back.
5. **If still uncertain:** Have Mom see her doctor about the bites. A dermatologist or PCP can rule out scabies, dermatitis, and several other causes in 10 minutes. Medicare covers it.

**If after 24 hours there is no physical evidence — pause everything.** Don't spend $1,000. Don't toss her dresser. Don't trash 80% of her wardrobe. Get the inspection or the doctor visit first.

## 1. WHY THE $1,000 EXTERMINATOR IS PROBABLY THE WRONG SPEND

Mom wants to spend $1,000 on an exterminator. **Ben is right that this is probably a bad call.** Here's why, in order of strength:

### 1.1 The exterminator treats rooms. He doesn't treat her stuff.

This is the single biggest reason. Even after a $1,000 chemical treatment of the bedroom, **Mom still has to do every single laundry/heat-treatment step in this plan.** The exterminator does not:

- Wash, dry, or heat-treat her clothes.
- Bag and seal her belongings.
- Inspect the closet contents.
- Encase the mattress.
- Place interceptor cups.
- Triage what to keep and what to dispose.

The EPA and every university extension service agree: **without prep (laundry, decluttering, encasements), professional treatment fails 40–60% of the time.** So $1,000 buys her at most 30–40% of the solution, and zero of the solution if she skips the laundry work — which is most of the work.

### 1.2 If she does the laundry/heat-treatment work, she probably doesn't need the exterminator at all

The dryer kills bed bugs. Sealed plastic contains them. Time finishes them. If Mom executes the heat-treatment workflow on her clothes/bedding, switches to a new encased mattress in the living room, places interceptor cups, and seals or disposes of the contaminated bedroom contents — **the structural treatment becomes optional.** Many people resolve mild-to-moderate infestations with just heat, encasements, and interceptors.

If after 6–8 weeks of monitoring she still has activity, *then* spend the $1,000. Not before.

### 1.3 Senior-targeted pest control fraud is a real, documented thing

Ben asked whether the exterminator might be lying to take her money. **Yes — this happens, and seniors are a known target.** AARP, the BBB, and state Attorneys General have warned about pest control scams targeting older homeowners and apartment-dwellers. Red flags to watch for:

- **Door-to-door "free inspection" offers.** Almost always followed by "we found something serious."
- **Pressure to sign today / "today only" pricing.**
- **Inability or refusal to show you the actual evidence** (live bug, fecal spotting, casts) on site.
- **Quotes that scale to perceived income** rather than the actual problem.
- **Recommending whole-home heat treatment ($2,500–$5,500) for a single bedroom** that hasn't been inspected.
- **No company name on the truck, no business card, no website, no EPA pesticide applicator license number.**
- **Verbal-only quotes; refusal to put scope of work in writing.**
- **Requests for cash or check made out to an individual** rather than a company.
- **Recommending "preventive" treatment quarterly forever** — bed bug treatment is event-based, not subscription-based.

If Mom got the $1,000 quote from someone who knocked on her door or who came up in conversation rather than a company she found through her own search of established local exterminators, the scam probability is meaningful. Ben should ask Mom: **"Where did you find this exterminator? Who recommended him? What's the company name? Can you look up reviews?"** Those four questions usually surface the truth fast.

### 1.4 The legitimate exterminator scenarios

Not every exterminator is a scammer. A legitimate situation might look like:

- The apartment's property management already had a confirmed infestation in another unit and has retained a contractor.
- Mom searched Google for "bed bug exterminator" + her town, called several, and got similar quotes.
- A trusted friend referred her.
- The company is BBB-accredited, named on a truck, and has online reviews.

Even in those cases — see 1.1. **The exterminator does not solve her clothing/bedding problem.** That work is hers regardless.

### 1.5 The bottom line

Spend the $1,000 only if:
1. Bed bugs are *confirmed* (Section 0), AND
2. The infestation is large/severe (15+ visible bugs, multiple rooms, weeks-old fecal spotting), AND
3. Mom has already completed the laundry/heat-treatment work and still sees activity after 6–8 weeks.

Otherwise: that's $1,000 better kept in her pocket — possibly toward something she actually needs, or saved against any future legitimate need.

## 4. THE LAUNDRY/HEAT-TREATMENT WORKFLOW (the actual work)

This is the workhorse. Done properly, this is what kills the bed bugs in her things.

### 4.1 The science (so Ben can verify)

- **30+ minutes on HIGH heat in a residential dryer** kills 100% of all bed bug life stages including eggs (sources: Cornell IPM, EPA, University of Minnesota Extension, Naylor & Boase research summarized by Virginia Tech entomology).
- **Dry first, don't wash first.** Wet items take 3× longer to reach kill temperature; eggs survive a 104°F wash.
- **Drum no more than half full** so items tumble freely and reach core temperature.
- 45 minutes is the recommended setting — gives a safety margin for thicker items (jeans, bedspreads).

### 4.2 The 14-step routine (Mom-facing)

> **LAUNDRY RUN — ONE LOAD AT A TIME**
>
> 1. Get TWO new black trash bags.
> 2. Put on bedroom outfit + booties (door card).
> 3. Open bedroom door, go in, close it.
> 4. Pick up about a grocery-bag's worth of clothes. Don't fill the trash bag full.
> 5. Put clothes in the FIRST black bag. Tie top tight.
> 6. Put that bag inside the SECOND black bag. Tie that one too.
> 7. Take off bedroom outfit → wall bag. Booties → wall bag.
> 8. Open door. Go out. Close it.
> 9. Walk straight to dryer. Don't put the bag on furniture along the way.
> 10. Open dryer. Open bag at the dryer mouth. Tip clothes in. Don't reach in.
> 11. Set dryer: HIGH HEAT, 45 MIN. Press START.
> 12. Take both empty bags straight to the outside trash NOW.
> 13. When dryer beeps: get a clear Ziploc. Tip hot clothes from dryer into Ziploc. Zip closed. Write today's date on it.
> 14. Carry Ziploc to the living room. Put on the "CLEAN" pile.
>
> **DONE when:** Sealed bag is on the clean pile and dryer is empty.
>
> Then shower and put on clean clothes.

### 4.3 What to heat-treat first (priorities for Days 4–7)

Enough to live on for two weeks:

- 7 days of underwear + socks
- 7 tops, 4 bottoms
- 2 pajama sets
- 2 sets of sheets, 2 pillowcases (the new ones arriving Wednesday count — they're already clean if they stay sealed in their packaging)
- 1 light blanket
- All current bath towels
- Whatever she's wearing today (after she showers, that goes in too)

Everything else stays sealed in the bedroom and gets processed over the following weeks at one load per day. No rush.

### 4.4 What can't go in the dryer

The photos show Mom has already gotten rid of most upholstered furniture and has been triaging hard. The remaining non-dryer-safe categories are short:

- **Boombox / small electronics:** Wipe exterior. Seal in a plastic bin for 18 months, OR with a Nuvan ProStrip for 2 weeks.
- **Shoes / sneakers:** Dryer rack (no tumbling), 30 min HIGH.
- **Wool, silk, "dry-clean only":** Dryer 90 min on medium/air-fluff (Cornell IPM allows lower heat with longer time).
- **Books, papers, photos** (only if essential): Sealed tote 18 months. Otherwise toss.
- **Old/stained low-value clothing or pillows:** Toss. Bag, label "BED BUGS," outdoor trash.
- **Cardboard boxes:** Always toss — bed bugs love cardboard. Replace with plastic bins if she needs storage.

Everything else — regular clothing, bedding, towels, soft toys — goes in the dryer per Section 4.2. **When in doubt, toss it.** Ben's mom has shown she's willing to declutter aggressively, and that's the right instinct here.

### 5.2 What gets heat-treated and kept

Per Section 4 — clothing she actively wears, bedding, towels, soft toys. Heat-treat in dryer, seal in Ziplocs, store in living room. That becomes her wardrobe.

### 5.3 What gets sealed and waited out

Short list — should be a small handful of items at most:

- **A few sentimental items, photos, papers** (only if she really wants to keep them): Sealed plastic tote, dated, labeled "DO NOT OPEN UNTIL [date + 18 months]."

If the list of "things to seal-and-wait" gets longer than ~5 items, she's keeping too much. Push back and toss more.

### 5.4 The carpet — five real options, ranked

The carpet is the one thing left. Here are the realistic options, in order of fit for Mom's situation:

#### Option A: Exile the bedroom (RECOMMENDED)

**This is the right answer for her.** Mom is moving permanently to the living room. She doesn't need the bedroom for anything. So instead of treating the carpet, **deny the bugs a food source until they die.**

- Close the bedroom door. Keep it closed.
- Towel under the door (gap blocker — bed bugs are crawling insects, no flight).
- Don't enter except to retrieve the few items she's heat-treating, via the bedroom-entry protocol (Section 3.3).
- Bed bugs starve in **4.5–6 months at room temperature** without a host (Polanco, Brewster & Miller, 2011, *Insects*; PMC4553461). Eggs hatch in 6–10 days, then those nymphs starve too. After **18 months sealed**, every life stage is dead — that's the safety margin.
- Place 2–4 ClimbUp interceptor cups loose on the bedroom floor near the door and along walls as passive monitors. Check monthly. Captures = activity. Zero captures for 60 consecutive days = high confidence the room is dying off.

**Cost: $0 (interceptor cups already in the shopping list). Effort: zero physical labor on the carpet itself. Time to all-clear: ~18 months.**

This is the right option when there's no need for the room and the operator is remote. The laziest option works because biology does the work.

#### Option B: Daily vacuum + desiccant dust

If Mom wants the bedroom usable sooner (4–8 weeks):

- Apply **CimeXa** (silica gel dust) along the carpet perimeter where it meets the baseboard — bed bugs' #1 hiding spot. Use a small bellows duster for thin even application. Wear N95.
- Daily vacuum the entire carpet, especially edges. Disposable bag, OR empty into sealed plastic bag and trash outside immediately.
- Continue 4–8 weeks until interceptor cups in the room show zero captures for 14 consecutive days.

**Cost: ~$25 (CimeXa). Effort: 15–30 min/day. Time to all-clear: 6–8 weeks.**

Daily for 4–8 weeks is real work; pace it. Weekly is a fallback if daily isn't sustainable, but less effective.

#### Option C: Steam treatment

Heat is the most reliable bed bug killer that exists. A consumer steamer with 160–180°F nozzle output kills all life stages on contact, even in carpet pile.

- Rent a steam cleaner from Home Depot (~$30–40/day) or buy one (McCulloch MC1275, ~$130, reaches 200°F+).
- Run slowly along all carpet edges and the entire surface — about 12 inches per 30 seconds.
- Repeat once a week for 3 weeks.

**Cost: $30–130. Effort: 2–3 hours per session, physically demanding — pace it across multiple days.**

#### Option D: Carpet removal

The nuclear option. Roll up the carpet + pad, bag them, mark "BED BUGS," and take to the dump (or arrange a junk-removal pickup).

- Eliminates the harborage entirely — carpet was the last fabric surface, this ends the bedroom problem.
- Apartment has subfloor underneath; can stay bare or get vinyl planks / new carpet later.
- **If she's renting, get landlord permission first.** Many leases require it.

**Cost: $200–500 (removal + replacement, or $0 if she leaves it bare). Effort: one afternoon. Time to all-clear: immediate.**

Worth strongly considering if the lease is ending soon or she's moving — why pay to keep a carpet she's leaving anyway?

#### Option E: EPA-registered carpet spray

Least-recommended. Many bed bugs are pyrethroid-resistant, so spray-only treatment is unreliable. If she wants a chemical add-on:

- Buy an EPA-registered product with "bed bugs" on the label (Bedlam Plus, Temprid Ready Spray).
- Apply along carpet edges per the label.
- **Pair with Option B or C — never as a standalone.**

**Cost: ~$25. Effort: light. Time to all-clear: not reliable alone.**

### 5.5 The recommendation

**Option A (exile) for the bedroom carpet, plus interceptor cups in the bedroom as passive monitors.**

- Mom moves to the living room anyway.
- Time and starvation do the work for $0.
- If 6 weeks pass with zero captures in the bedroom interceptors AND zero captures in the living-room interceptors AND zero new bites → very high confidence the problem is contained without spending another dollar.
- If at 6 weeks there's evidence of continued activity, escalate to Option C (steam) or D (carpet removal) — armed with actual evidence rather than guessing.

This pairs perfectly with the "don't pay $1,000 for the exterminator" argument: between exile + heat-treat + interceptors, she's spending ~$95 to solve a problem the exterminator was going to solve for $1,000 and still leave her doing the laundry.
